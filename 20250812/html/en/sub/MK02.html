The Need for Carbon Neutrality and Sustainable Energy Solutions
2025.05.20
Global climate and environmental experts gathered in Jeju to discuss solutions for carbon neutrality and a sustainable future. On May 29th, at the Jeju International Convention Center, a session themed 'Challenges and Opportunities for Achieving Carbon Neutrality and Energy Transition: Leading Climate Action and Sustainability' was successfully held as part of the 20th Jeju Forum for Peace and Prosperity.

This session was co-hosted by the Jeju Peace Institute and Jeju Special Self-Governing Province to share practical policy directions and innovative technologies for responding to the accelerating climate crisis and to explore global cooperation plans.

In his welcoming address, Jeju Governor Oh Young-hoon stated, "Jeju has set the goal of achieving carbon neutrality by 2035, 15 years ahead of the government's target," and announced, "We will create new economic models and climate solutions through sustainable energy policies." This is well demonstrated by Jeju's milestone of covering all electricity with renewable energy during peak consumption hours.

Armida Alisjahbana, Executive Secretary of the United Nations Economic and Social Commission for Asia and the Pacific (UN ESCAP), praised this achievement in her video address, emphasizing that "Jeju has shown a credible pathway to achieving net zero." She also pointed out that globally, greenhouse gas reduction challenges remain in the industrial, transportation, and energy sectors.

In the following discussion, moderated by Kim Ju-jin, CEO of Solutions for Our Climate, domestic and international experts participated as panelists, presenting various policy and technological solutions. Ryu Hak-seok, Director of the Global Green Growth Institute, emphasized the need for international cooperation, stating, "As Asia is at the center of energy consumption and carbon emissions, it has significant responsibility and role in addressing the climate crisis."

Kang Jin-young, Head of Policy Research at Jeju Research Institute, explained, "Due to Jeju's geological characteristics, it is sensitive to climate crisis, enabling rapid policy implementation," and introduced Jeju's proactive efforts to secure resident acceptance and power system stability, including benefit-sharing systems and distributed energy special zones.

Darren Southcott, Visiting Professor at Jeju National University, mentioned the role of civil society organizations, stating, "Rather than waiting for technology, citizens' actions can accelerate climate policies," citing the Gotjawal protection movement as an example.

Peter van Hendriksen, Professor at the Technical University of Denmark, argued, "Water electrolysis technology is in its early stages with high cost burden," and emphasized the need to secure the feasibility of technology adoption through economic incentives such as carbon taxes and strengthened producer responsibility.

Participants agreed on the need for awareness raising, practical action, and large-scale investment for achieving carbon neutrality, and reached consensus that stakeholders from various sectors should work closely together to overcome the climate crisis.

Through this forum, Jeju is evaluated as having created an opportunity to present practical directions for climate action to the international community as an exemplary city of sustainability and innovation.
